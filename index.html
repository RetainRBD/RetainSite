Paid Media

The Ad Is A Promise.
Everything Downstream
Depends On Keeping It.

Most brands treat paid media like a faucet. Turn it up and revenue goes up. That's not a business. That's a dependency. We build acquisition systems that get more efficient as they scale not less.

The creative angle you run determines the customer who clicks. The customer who clicks determines who lands on your site. Who lands on your site determines your subscription rate, your LTV, and your churn. The entire downstream performance of your business has a direct line back to the promise in the ad.

Most brands run one type of creative for every stage of the funnel. Someone who has never heard of you gets the same ad as someone who has been to your site three times. That's why CAC stays high and MER compresses as you scale. We structure creative around where someone actually is in their relationship with your brand and what they need to see at that specific moment to move forward.

We don't recommend scaling spend until the conversion and retention system is ready to receive it. When it is, scaling looks completely different. MER improves instead of compressing. CAC drops without reducing spend. That only happens when the ad, the site, and the lifecycle are all aligned.

Website: The Commitment Architecture

Most Sites Are Built
To Get A Sale.
We Build Them To Get A Commitment.

A sale is a transaction. A commitment is a decision. The customer who commits understands the product, believes in the outcome, and buys into the duration. That customer stays. That customer refers. That customer is the foundation of a business that compounds.

Every element of a product page is either moving someone toward a commitment or pulling them toward a one-time transaction. Most brands never think about it this way. They optimize for conversion rate. We optimize for the quality of the conversion.

The biggest lever most brands never pull is message match. When the ad promises one thing and the landing page delivers another, conversion dies in that gap. The brain registers the disconnect before the conscious mind does. The page has to continue the exact conversation the ad started. Not a related one. The same one.

We built our own framework for evaluating and rebuilding product pages. Four stages. Non-negotiable sequence. Signal, Proof, Commit, Continue. Every site we audit gets mapped against each one in order, because you cannot fix stage three before stage two is working.

Stage 01
Signal
Three seconds to know this is for them. The headline names the transformation, not the product. The imagery shows context, not just the product. A stranger should be able to describe what this page sells in three seconds or it is leaking traffic before a single word is read.
Stage 02
Proof
Belief before price. For most DTC categories the customer has been disappointed before. That skepticism lives in their body when they land. Proof disarms it with specificity. Reviews at the right stage of the journey. Timeline education that pre-frames retention before the first purchase.
Stage 03
Commit
Subscription is not a discount. It is the way the product is meant to be used. When it is framed that way, the subscription rate changes dramatically. Not because you offered more savings but because you offered more sense. The offer has to make commitment feel like the obvious logical choice.
Stage 04
Continue
The first purchase is the beginning of a relationship, not the completion of a transaction. The page shows what happens next. The product ecosystem, the community, the transformation arc. Buying once should feel like the entry point, not the finish line.
The Dependency

How the offer is framed on the product page determines first-purchase subscription rate. That single metric has a direct line to LTV trajectory, sustainable CAC, and your ability to scale spend without compressing MER. The entire chain starts with copy on a product page.

Email & Lifecycle

Flows Are The Foundation.
Campaigns Are The
Ongoing Relationship.

If your lifecycle is healthy and we're talking flows alone, not campaigns it should generate 30 to 40 percent of your total monthly revenue automatically. Most brands are leaving that money on the table every single month.

The most critical moment in a subscription customer's lifecycle is day 14. By then the novelty has worn off. They're using the product and forming an opinion about whether it works. If the onboarding flow hasn't proactively addressed this moment set timeline expectations, reinforced the habit, connected them to a community this is when the decision to cancel is made. The actual cancellation might happen at day 30. The decision happened at day 14.

Most brands think about email as a channel for selling. We think about it as a system for building habit. The difference shows up in churn rate. A customer who has a habit doesn't comparison shop. Doesn't need a coupon. Comes back because the routine is already built.

We build two versions of every lifecycle one for subscription brands and one for non-subscription. The philosophy is the same. The timing, triggers, and messaging differ based on the product's natural usage cycle. Not a content calendar. The actual rhythm of how someone uses the product.
